Yield estimates, temperature ranges, rainfall bands, elevation thresholds—these appear as "hard" data but are actually approximations that don't account for the interaction of different climate factors, influence of varieties, microbiological and soil variation, or more subtle influences like positioning of earth-energy-lines. The quantitative data should be held lightly, presented as rough senses rather than precise specifications: "feeds a household," "abundant seasonal," "modest but steady."

Anecdotal reports—"several different people said something grew easily and effortlessly somewhere"—are often more valuable than formal data, because they describe what actually happened in real conditions. Traditional Hawaiian knowledge carries similar weight: observations refined over centuries of careful attention to how plants and people relate to specific places.

The schema must accommodate both without the "hard" overriding the "soft." A plant record might carry formal data (temperature range, rainfall requirements, yield estimates) alongside qualitative observations (how it tastes, what it needs to be prepared, what it's like to live with) and successional attributes (sun/shade, pioneer/closer, what it pairs well with). The basic view presents a concise voice; the detailed view opens into prose with the Prep/Set/Forget beats.
