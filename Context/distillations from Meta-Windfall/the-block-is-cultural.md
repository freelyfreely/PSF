The main obstacle to food sovereignty on Hawaiʻi is not a lack of data but the inertia of habits, worldviews, and social conventions. The knowledge exists—in traditional Hawaiian practice, in syntropic agroforestry, in the lived experience of local gardeners and farmers. What is missing is the cultural container that makes this knowledge actionable and desirable.

Hawaiʻi now imports over 90% of its food, though for most of its history it provided 100% and even supplied passing ships. This dependency is not inevitable but constructed—maintained by a "landscaping-gaze" that treats land as a dead static rectangle to impose fixed ideas upon, rather than as a living meta-organism evolving across time. The project works on this level: bringing deep ecological knowledge into a form that is accessible, practical, and inspiring, so that the shift from passive consumer to active participant in one's own food system becomes not just possible but obvious.

The design and voice carry as much of this work as the data. A beautifully designed recommendation that says *why* a plant fits your rainfall and temperature, that names its light needs and succession role in prose that makes you think "of course, that makes perfect sense"—this is already the deeper gestalt working, without a lecture you won't relate to.
