We do not plant a forest; we provoke a sequence. The degraded soil is not a void to be filled but a history of exhaustion that requires a specific entry point: the pioneer. These early plants—grasses, manioc, fire-sensitive shrubs—are not the goal; they are the medicine that the land must take. They are brought in to suffer the conditions that the long-term species cannot yet endure: the acidity, the aluminum, the lack of structure.

But there is a danger in success. When the pioneer does its job—accumulating biomass, changing the pH, inviting the worms—it begins to mature. If it is allowed to stay, it shifts from being a stimulant to a suppressant. The vigor it brought to the system calcifies into dominance. Therefore, we must be willing to interrupt the very plants that saved the soil. The pioneer must be pruned back or removed not because it failed, but because it succeeded enough to make way for the next stage.

This is the "sacrifice of the pioneer." The manioc and the grasses are the catalysts that die to birth the *Corindiba* and the *Imbauba*. These, in turn, are cut back to birth the cacao and the timber. To the casual observer, a plot that is being cut back might look like destruction or a return to poverty. To us, it is the rhythm of breathing out. We are not fighting against nature’s tendency to scrub; we are simply accelerating the rhythm so that the succession moves faster than the weeds of degradation. The system requires a constant, managed senescence of the "now" to feed the "next."
