The people who will consult this tool are diverse: local-kine folks, transplants, landscapers, perhaps policy-makers. Very few are ecologists; most have never heard of "succession." But they are not empty vessels waiting to be filled with data. They are people with relationships to their land—some inherited, some newly forming, some broken by displacement or neglect.

The audience includes those who have never grown food but might put chili peppers near their kitchen door. It includes those who already garden but see their plot as a rectangle rather than a community. It includes those who remember when Hawaiʻi fed itself and want to help that memory become future. It includes Tam's political constituents—people who respond to "Local Food, Local Power" as a platform because it names something they already feel.

The tool must speak to all of these without condescension or dilution. It must be trustworthy enough for someone to stake their garden on, and inspiring enough to shift their relationship to land. The inner succession—the change that happens when someone starts harvesting from their own ground—is the real product. The plant recommendations are the delivery mechanism for that shift.
