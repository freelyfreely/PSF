Tam's trinity—Prep, Set, Forget—is not just a catchy project name but the actual structure through which knowledge passes from the field to the person. **Prep** names what the site needs before this plant goes in: sun or shade, spacing, what to put nearby. **Set** names how to get it in the ground: propagation, timing, water needs at establishment. **Forget** names what happens after: how fast it takes off, when it yields, what it needs from you (almost nothing, if the syntropic logic is right).

The genius is in the third beat. "Forget" is not negligence—it is the recognition that properly positioned plants, embedded in their successional context, become part of a living system that maintains itself. The practitioner's role shifts from constant intervention to strategic presence: knowing when to prune, when to harvest, when to let the pioneer fulfill its function and make way for what comes next. The knowledge that matters is not abstract data but intimate familiarity with one's own ground—which plants thrive where, which combinations generate vigor, which moments call for cutting back.

This structure also carries the project's ethical claim: that anyone, anywhere, can begin. The cassava can wait. The chili peppers near the kitchen door are enough to start the inner succession—the shift from seeing land as inert substrate to experiencing it as a community of beings whose interactions, when supported, generate abundance.
