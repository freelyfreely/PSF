# Succession as Method

The land recovers itself. On abandoned pasture in southern Bahia, acidic, stripped soils rebuilt into productive agroforests within five to eight years — no fertilizers, no machinery. The mechanism: species succession.

The critical discovery emerged through failure and attention. Cultivars struggled where native pioneers grew vigorously. Selective weeding — removing only mature grasses, allowing native herbs and trees their soil-improving function — became the first principle. Pruning revealed itself as the key intervention: cut back maturing pioneers, and the entire community invigorates. A plant in vigorous phase stimulates its neighbors; a senescing plant induces decline.

**The critical factor determining health and productivity was not initial soil quality but the composition, density, and timing of the plant community.** Poorer plots forced high-density planting in successional order — and became the most productive. Four species planted in natural sequence grew vigorously together; the same four planted simultaneously struggled. **The critical factor for establishment is not light but the order and timing of introduction within natural succession.**

## The Praxis

1. Identify optimal species and successions for the site; plant in natural groupings.
2. Maximize biodiversity, filling every niche — including spontaneous natives.
3. Optimize timing so each species finds conditions to establish and drive the community.
4. **Accelerate succession through pruning** — rejuvenating maturing plants, controlling light and space individually.

Pruning is the primary intervention: accelerated growth, mulch, improved soil texture, directed succession. Periodic rejuvenation extends pioneers' useful life.

## The Systems That Preceded This Discovery

This was recovery, not invention:

**The frijolar** (Maya-descended Central America): Beneath sacred *Ceiba pentandra*, dense leguminous trees (40% *Inga*), heavily pruned. When the ceiba loses leaves, beans and corn are broadcast into mowed herbaceous layer and fresh mulch. Yields exceeded 2,100 kg/ha beans, 1,500 kg/ha corn — far above slash-and-burn. Functioned by strategic pruning, cultivars matching natural conditions, timing synchronized to the ceiba's rhythm.

**Traditional polycrop coffee** (Central America, Colombia): Deep-rooted canopy trees, dense *Inga* and *Erythrina* pruned annually to trunk, banana, citrus, coffee pruned simultaneously. Organic material spread and cut small. Six weeks later: new growth and prolific flowering. High yields for decades without fertilizers. No erosion. Diseases insignificant.

Success rests on: deep-rooted canopy trees capturing subsoil minerals; leguminous trees for shade and soil improvement; pruning synchronized to crops' need for light then shade; rigorous cutback simulating forest clearing; species chosen for synergetic interactions.

## What Conventional Agriculture Displaces

Slash-and-burn and clearing weeding accelerate mineralization and eliminate soil-recovering pioneers, causing fertility setback. Modern "shade coffee" in static configuration still requires imported inputs. Plants positioned where their ecophysiology would not place them attract pests that remove them — contributing to the life-increase the system's managers failed to provide.

Fertilizers, pesticides, and clearing weeding are not sustainable solutions but sabotage of the effort toward sustainable food production.

## The Ground

What this establishes is not a technique but a stance: **agriculture succeeds when it enters the stream of species succession and is carried along by it.** The practitioner's role is to observe, synchronize, and make strategic interventions at the right moments. The soil is a living system that recovers when given conditions. The farm is a community of beings whose interactions, properly supported, generate abundance.

No external input is needed. The knowledge required is intimate familiarity with local flora — held by older rural communities and traditional farmers. The method works wherever crops can be cultivated, provided the practitioner understands the local biotope, identifies cultivar niches, and grasps their interactions with the full community. Seventeen years of unsubsidized free-market practice produced this. The claim is universal: the principles work, require no extra investment, and any movement in this direction benefits farmer, consumer, and ecosystem simultaneously. The knowledge belongs to no one and serves everyone.
