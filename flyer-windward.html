On the Windward Coast

Eight plants that will feed you here, in the order you would plant them.

Hāmākua, Hilo, Puna.90 to 130 inches a year.

Mīkana

Papaya

Carica papaya

Fruit sub-canopy wet, drained warm, no frost to 3,000 ft sun, sheltered

Fruit in six months from a seed, which nothing else here can say. It is also finished by year four. A fast bright thing that fills the gap while the trees are still coming.

13 510 50

Prep

Deep ground with humus, drained and aerated. Standing water kills it in days. Shelter from wind: the trunk is hollow and it goes over. Sun.

Set

Fresh seed. Plant several. They come up male, female, or both, and one male covers six females. Fruit at six months.

Forget

Fruits year-round and peaks in years two and three. By year four it is falling off. Take it green as a vegetable or ripe as a fruit. When it is spent, cut it down; the next one is already up.

Kalo

Taro

Colocasia esculenta

Corm — starch ground wet, standing warm to 8,900 ft sun to light shade

Water and time. Seven to ten months, and it wants the wet this coast already has, standing or running. Under a canopy that closes, it keeps going where the sun plants stop.

13 510 50

Prep

Heavy, fertile ground that holds water. Flooded ground grows it best by a factor of six. Sun, or the light shade under something taller.

Set

A corm, a corm piece, or a side sucker, two to three inches deep. Seed will not come true and nobody uses it.

Forget

Corms at seven to ten months. The leaves come the whole way through and get cut as a green. Nothing on this plant is eaten raw, ever. Cook it, and it is the staple this island ran on.

ʻAwapuhi

Ginger

Zingiber officinale

Rhizome — spice ground wet warm to 6,200 ft sun to part shade

Wet ground and a shaded floor, which is what this coast is. Ten months in, the stalks wither, and that withering is the harvest telling you it is finished.

13 510 50

Prep

Well-drained ground with humus in it. Sun or part shade. It wants the rain this coast already gets.

Set

Split a rhizome at the first sign of new growth and plant the pieces.

Forget

A ten-month season. Harvest when the stalks begin to wither. On any slope, plant something with it: bare ground under ginger erodes badly.

Maiʻa

Banana

Musa spp.

Fruit — starch sub-canopy wet warm, no frost to 4,000 ft sun, sheltered

A windbreak that feeds you. The stem fruits once and dies, and the mat it stands in has already replaced it. What looks like one plant is a queue.

13 510 50

Prep

Fertile ground that drains, in sun, out of the wind. It wants the rain this coast gives.

Set

A sucker off an established mat. Not seed. Somebody will give you one.

Forget

A stem fruits at twelve to eighteen months, then dies, and the next sucker is already up. Cut the spent stem and drop it where it stands: that is the mulch. Keep two or three followers per mat and cut the rest.

Lilikoʻi

Passion fruit

Passiflora edulis

Fruit vine wet, drained warm to 3,000 ft dappled shade

A vine that wants dappled shade, which means the edge of a forest rather than a fence in the open. Fruit in a year from a cutting. It lives three to six years, hard and fast, and then it is done.

13 510 50

Prep

Humus-rich ground that drains. Do not feed it: rich ground makes leaf and no fruit. Dappled shade, sheltered from cold wind. It needs something to climb.

Set

A cutting fruits the same year. Seed takes one to two years and can take twelve months just to germinate.

Forget

Two crops a year. The fruit tells you it is ready by wrinkling and falling. Pick it off the ground. Cut the whole vine to the ground when it tires and it comes back.

Ice cream bean

Inga edulis

Pods — sweet · nitrogen canopy wet warm, no frost to 7,200 ft sun to dappled shade

The nurse tree that fruits. It fixes nitrogen, drops high-nitrogen leaf, casts the shade cacao and coffee want, and hands you a three-foot pod full of something like vanilla ice cream. Cut it back and it comes back harder.

13 510 50

Prep

Almost any ground, including worn-out ground and limestone. Sun, or the dappled shade under something else.

Set

Fresh seed, which germinates in days and cannot be stored. Plant out at four to five months.

Forget

First pods at two years. It flowers and fruits year-round. Coppice it and it resprouts, and everything you cut off it becomes nitrogen on the ground for whatever it is nursing.

Gliricidia

Gliricidia sepium

No food — nitrogen, mulch, shade canopy anything warm, no frost to 5,200 ft sun

It is here to work. A branch cut off it and pushed in the ground becomes a tree. Cut it twice a year and everything near it grows faster. Toxic to people, dogs, and horses — this one is not dinner.

13 510 50

Prep

Any ground: acid, alkaline, worn out, waterlogged, or a 40% slope. Full sun.

Set

A large cutting. A branch, pushed into the ground. It is the easiest living fence there is. Seed germinates in a week at nearly 100%.

Forget

Ten feet a year once it starts. Pollard it above head height and cut it back every two to three years, or lop it as often as you like. It resprouts every time. The leaf you cut is the nitrogen. Keep it away from the dog.

ʻUlu

Breadfruit

Artocarpus altilis

Fruit — starch canopy wet warm, no frost to 2,100 ft sun

A tree that makes bread. Grafted, it starts fruiting around year three and keeps going past a person's lifetime: fifty to two hundred fruits a season, twice a year, from ground that gets nothing but its own fallen leaves.

13 510 50

Prep

Wet, warm, low ground, and no frost, ever. Drainage is the one thing it will not forgive. Thirty feet of room. Roots run shallow and wide, so the digging under it stops once it is in.

Set

Grafted, not seed: seed takes ten years and comes up a stranger. Goes in during the rains. Wants shade for its first year or two, and a banana on the windward side gives it that and takes the wind off besides.

Forget

Fruits May to October, and again. The leaves are the size of a doormat and fall all year. That is the mulch, and it stays where it lands. Tip-prune once a year and the fruit stays in reach. By year five the shade underneath is deep, and that is when the kalo goes in.

Eight is what fits on a sheet of paper. Your ground has more in it than eight plants, and it is not the same as your neighbour's ground. The site reads the rain, the elevation and the heat where you actually stand, and tells you what that means.
