# Eugenia brasiliensis — raw

**Identity:** grumichama, Brazilian cherry (ambiguous — cf. Surinam cherry). Myrtaceae. PSF role:
sub-canopy, fruit. Copy entry #37. Kin to Surinam cherry (#33) — same genus, and the **well-behaved**
one: it stays where it is planted.

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 1 · slow sub-canopy fruit tree** · lifespan long (slow-growing,
decades; first fruit ~4 yr). Tidy + non-invasive — a secondary that needs patience, not management.

## Climate & site — by source
- **Rainfall** — Ferns: preferred **1,200–2,500 mm → 47–98 in**; tolerated **800–3,500 mm → 31–138 in**
  (copy's 30–138 tracks).
- **Temperature** — Ferns: preferred **21–32 °C → 70–90 °F**; tolerated **15–38 °C → 59–100 °F**; mature
  trees to ~−3 °C (27 °F) briefly.
- **Altitude** — Ferns: **optimal 90 m or less → ~295 ft** — a genuinely **lowland** tree (copy's
  "coast to 300 ft" tracks exactly).
- **Soil / pH** — Ferns: medium-acid, high-organic; **intolerant of alkaline ground.** Shelter from
  strong wind; adverse to prolonged drought.
- **Light** — Ferns: full sun or part-day shade.

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: seed loses viability in ~6 wk + germinates slowly; cuttings + air layering easy. First fruit
  ~4 yr. In the subtropics: spring flowers, fruit ~1 mo later, harvest over a few days; in the tropics
  it spreads over months. Slow, 25–50 ft (usually less).

## Uses / food — by source
- Ferns: dark crimson-to-black cherry ~1 in, soft melting pulp tasting much like a true cherry (flavour
  mostly in the skin); raw or jam/jelly. Red + yellow forms; red sweetest. Persistent little sepals the
  only nuisance.

## Hazards — by source
- Ferns: none reported. **No weediness, no toxicity** — the contrast to its Surinam-cherry cousin is
  the point.

## Inferences
- The genuinely lowland altitude (<300 ft) is the tight constraint — a coast plant, not an upland one.
  On a list that keeps hitting invasives, worth flagging the ones that are simply well-behaved.

## Anecdotes
- Brazilian Atlantic-coast Eugenia; a collector's + dooryard fruit here, grown by people who want a
  cherry that will actually fruit in the tropics.
