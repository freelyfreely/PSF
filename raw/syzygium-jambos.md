# Syzygium jambos — raw

**Identity:** rose apple, plum rose, malabar plum. Myrtaceae. PSF role: sub-canopy, fruit. Copy
entry #35. Kin to ʻōhiʻa ʻai (#13) and jamun (#34).

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 1 · coppicing sub-canopy tree** · lifespan long (medium growth,
fast once older; first fruit ~4 yr, coppices hard — 3 m sprouts within a year of cutting). The
vigorous resprouting is exactly what makes it both a useful biomass tree **and** a wet-forest weed —
an **arrested-state risk** (cf. [INDICATORS.md](../INDICATORS.md) IV).

## Climate & site — by source
- **Rainfall** — Ferns: preferred **1,200–1,600 mm → 47–63 in**; tolerated **700–4,000 mm → 28–157 in**
  (copy's 28–157 tracks). Dislikes a dry season unless the soil holds moisture.
- **Temperature** — Ferns: optimal **18–38 °C → 64–100 °F**; tolerated **5–40 °C → 41–104 °F**; mature
  trees to ~−3 °C (27 °F).
- **Altitude** — Ferns: typical 600–1,400 m, max to 2,300 m, usual ceiling below **1,200 m → ~3,900 ft**
  (copy's 3,900 ft tracks).
- **Soil / pH** — Ferns: any reasonable soil; tolerates poor, sandy, **alkaline** (slow + poor-fruiting
  there); pH **5.5–7**, tolerated 5–8.
- **Light** — Ferns: full sun or part shade; young plants need shade.

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: seed short-lived — **one seed throws 3–8 mostly-true seedlings** (polyembryonic); air
  layering works; transplants poorly (pot early). First fruit ~4 yr (esp. from layers). Coppices
  vigorously.

## Uses / food — by source
- Ferns: pale, hollow, crisp fruit to ~2 in, sweet + faintly perfumed, **tasting distinctly of roses**;
  bruises fast → eaten fresh, jelly, or distilled for rosewater. Flowers candied.

## Hazards — by source
- Ferns: **roots, bark, and leaves carry an alkaloid + trace cyanide; the roots are considered
  poisonous.** The **fruit is the safe, eaten part** — named plainly.
- **Invasive note:** naturalised + weedy in wet regions, forms dense streambank stands in Puna + Hilo.
  Site where it will be managed, never near intact forest.

## Inferences
- The coppicing vigour is the double message: a genuine chop-and-drop biomass tree in a managed guild,
  and a forest-smothering stall in the wild. Its own successional lesson — a secondary with no
  successor beneath it becomes an arrested state.

## Anecdotes
- SE Asian, carried everywhere as ornamental + dooryard fruit; in Hawaiʻi it slipped into wet forest
  and became a manager's problem — the guava note again (lovely, easy, edible, a real threat).
