# Artocarpus heterophyllus — raw

**Identity:** jackfruit. Moraceae. PSF role: canopy, fruit/starch. Copy entry #28. Kin to ʻulu
(#12) and cempedak (#29) — the same wet-low-ground genus; the biggest tree-borne fruit there is.

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 2 · canopy fruit/starch tree** · lifespan long (decades, to a
century; first fruit 3–8 yr, ~8 typical from seed). A slow, large canopy member that wants shade
young and accumulated life beneath it — like ʻulu, a structural tree, not a placenta.

## Climate & site — by source
- **Rainfall** — Ferns: **900–4,000 mm → 35–157 in**, evenly distributed; prefers no dry season,
  tolerates a short one.
- **Temperature** — Ferns: preferred **24–28 °C → 75–82 °F**; mean max 32–35 °C, mean min 16–20 °C
  (**61–68 °F**). Frost-free.
- **Altitude** — Ferns: below **1,000 m → ~3,300 ft** preferred; native 450–1,200 m.
- **Soil / pH** — Ferns: deep, well-drained alluvial; pH **5.5–7.5**, tolerated 4.3–8.
- **Light** — Ferns: young plants need partial shade, mature trees increasing light. Mature trees take
  some drought.

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: seed 80–100 % up in ~5–6 wk but **loses viability fast — sow fresh**; deep taproot,
  transplants badly (pot deep, plant early). Root cuttings + air layering work; grafted trees fruit
  sooner + stay smaller. First crop 3–8 yr; ~25 fruits at peak (5–6 yr on).

## Uses / food — by source
- Ferns: two foods — green flesh cooked as a vegetable (the "pulled-pork" use); ripe flesh intensely
  sweet (to 500 pods per fruit); seeds boiled/roasted like chestnuts (real starch + protein). Fruit to
  ~45 lb. **Copious latex glues everything — oil hands + blade.**

## Hazards — by source
- Ferns: none as a food (the latex is a nuisance, not a toxin).

## Inferences
- Same niche as ʻulu but larger-fruited and slower to a market of one household — the ripe fruit
  problem (too big, too strong) is why the green-fruit trade is what actually moves it.

## Anecdotes
- South Indian, ancient; one tree can carry ~3 t of fruit a year. Came with plantation-era migration,
  sat as a curiosity until the pulled-jackfruit trend gave the green fruit a market.
