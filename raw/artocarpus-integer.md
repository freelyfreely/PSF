# Artocarpus integer — raw

**Identity:** cempedak, chempedak. Moraceae. PSF role: canopy, fruit. Copy entry #29. The
jackfruit's smaller, richer relative — for the hottest, wettest, lowest ground.

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 2 · canopy fruit tree** · lifespan long (decades; first fruit
3–6 yr from seed, 2–4 grafted). A large structural canopy fruit, shade-demanding when young — ʻulu /
jackfruit class.

## Climate & site — by source
- **Rainfall** — Ferns: preferred **2,200–3,500 mm → 87–138 in**; tolerated **1,250–4,500 mm →
  49–177 in**. Wants no distinct dry season.
- **Temperature** — Ferns: preferred **22–38 °C → 72–100 °F**; tolerated **16–47 °C → 61–117 °F**.
- **Altitude** — Ferns: optimal below 1,200 m, occasional to **1,200 m → ~3,900 ft**. **The copy's
  "coast to 1,500 ft, best under 450" is a conservative narrowing** — reasonable for cempedak's
  cold-sensitivity, but flag that Ferns allows to 1,200 m; the 450 figure reads as *metres* misused as
  a low ceiling, worth a steward check.
- **Soil / pH** — Ferns: deep, well-drained, fertile; pH **4.5–6**, tolerated 4–6.5. **Unusually takes
  a high water table + periodic flooding**, even acid swamp water.
- **Light** — Ferns: young plants need shade, mature ones light.

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: seed sown fresh + warm (~75 % up); direct or deep containers. Root cuttings + air layering
  work; clonal trees fruit 2–4 yr vs 3–6 from seed. Tree 30–80 ft.

## Uses / food — by source
- Ferns: golden, soft, strongly fragrant pulp — sweet, durian-crossed-with-mango; raw, fried, or
  pudding; unripe fruit in soup as a vegetable; seeds roasted/boiled to a nutty snack.

## Hazards — by source
- Ferns: none as a food.

## Inferences
- The narrowest heat/wet/low envelope on the fruit list — a genuinely windward-lowland tree. Keep it
  off any upland recommendation; it is fussier about cold than ʻulu.

## Anecdotes
- Malay + Bornean; a dooryard and forest-edge tree across insular SE Asia. Rarer here than jackfruit,
  living mostly in the yards of people who knew it before they came — a fruit precious to a specific
  community and invisible to everyone else.
