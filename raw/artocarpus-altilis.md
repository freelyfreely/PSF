# Artocarpus altilis — raw

**Identity:** ʻulu, breadfruit. Moraceae. PSF role: canopy / fruit (starch). Copy entry #12.
The keystone of the wet-coast guild — an unbroken line of cuttings back to New Guinea.

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 2** · lifespan **50–100 yr**. Slow to establish (three years),
needs accumulated life beneath it — the canopy the guild is built around, not a pioneer.

## Climate & site — by source
- **Rainfall** — Ferns: preferred **1,500–2,500 mm → 59–98 in**; tolerated **exceptionally to 3,000 mm
  → 118 in**. Elevitch/Ragone: *"1,500–3,000 mm (60–120 in), but yields on atolls at 1,000 mm (40 in)."*
  **Highest-tier for THIS island — Mausio, Miura & Lincoln (2020), *PLoS ONE* 15(5):e0228552**, an
  EcoCrop suitability model **empirically re-calibrated against 1,200 naturalized breadfruit trees mapped
  in Hawaiʻi.** Table 1 refined precipitation parameters: **optimal 1,500–4,000 mm (59–157 in), absolute
  750–8,000 mm (30–315 in).** The paper explicitly found the generic EcoCrop bounds *"highly restrictive
  compared to the distribution of naturalized trees surveyed in Hawaiʻi, especially in terms of rainfall"*
  and **expanded the upper threshold** (its prior absolute max was 3,500 mm). **Finding — resolved, and
  it inverts the earlier one.** The shipped **157 in was correct after all** — it matches Mausio's optimal
  upper (4,000 mm) exactly. The day-1 flag was right that it was *unsourced* (and it did coincide with
  ginger's/inga's 157), but the resolution is to **source it, not delete it**: a Hawaiʻi-calibrated
  empirical model outranks generic tropical profiles for this island's bands (see SOURCES.md). **Applied
  to `site.html`/copy #12 (session 6):** preferred `rain:[60,157]` (Mausio optimal), tolerance
  `rainTol:[30,315]` (Mausio absolute) — an *expansion* past the old unsourced `[40,180]`, per the
  expand-when-justifiable rule. Ferns/Elevitch remain the lower-rainfall corroboration (atoll yields at
  40 in sit inside the new absolute floor of 30 in).
- **Temperature** — Ferns: preferred **21–32 °C → 70–90 °F**; tolerated **12–40 °C → 54–104 °F**;
  **injured below 5 °C → 41 °F**. **Elevitch/Ragone** agrees closely: mean annual 15–40 °C (59–104 °F),
  best 21–32 °C (70–90 °F); minimum tolerated 5–10 °C (41–50 °F). (Shipped "below about 41°F the leaves
  burn", confirmed. No frost.)
- **Altitude** — Ferns: optimal **below 650 m → ~2,130 ft**; tolerated to **1,550 m → ~5,080 ft**.
  **Elevitch/Ragone:** *"Elevation range 0–1,500 m (0–5,100 ft); best below 600–650 m (2,000–2,160
  ft)."* Confirms both figures. (Shipped `coast to 2,100 ft` = the ~650 m production optimum; copy
  notes the "<650" and wider figures both, "the gap is where most of this island sits.")
- **Soil** — Ferns: deep, fertile, **well-drained** — drainage is the one thing it will not forgive.
  No pH given by Ferns; **Elevitch/Ragone supplies it: pH 6.1–7.4**, light-to-medium freely-draining
  soils, tolerates saline and coralline atoll soils. (Fills the gap Ferns left.)
- **Light** — Ferns: **young plants need some shade, more light as they mature** (the banana-windbreak
  role, copy).

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: seed germinates 85% in ~2 weeks at 24–27 °C, field-plantable at ~1 year; **seed-grown crops
  at 3–6 years.** But the seedless Hawaiian ʻulu is **grafted / root-shoot** — seed "takes 5–10 years
  and comes up a stranger" (copy); a vegetative tree fruits in 2–5. (Shipped `first food year 3`.)
- Fruiting: **two crops most years** (Ferns); copy gives May–October then again.

## Uses / food — by source
- Green and firm: cooks like a potato. Ripe: sweet, roasts to caramel. Never raw. Milky sap = wait,
  watery = pick now. Seeds (on seeded kinds) cook like chestnuts.

## Hazards — by source
- None of note (cooked).

## Inferences
- Doormat-sized leaves drop year-round = the mulch, in place. Deep shade by ~year 5 = when kalo/cacao
  go in. *[Inference from habit; the canopy→understory timing is a guild-pass claim.]*

## Anecdotes
- Polynesian ʻulu is a near-clonal genetic bottleneck — every tree carried by hand. Later loaded onto
  the *Bounty* to feed enslaved people cheaply. "Fed people who chose it and people given no choice."
  *[Ethnohistorical — texture.]*
