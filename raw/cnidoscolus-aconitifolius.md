# Cnidoscolus aconitifolius — raw

**Identity:** chaya, tree spinach, chaya mansa. Euphorbiaceae. PSF role: shrub, leaf (protein).
Copy entry #21. The Maya dooryard green — kin in *role* to katuk (#54) and aibika (#20): plant once,
cut for years.

**Succession (axis 2):** **secondary 1 · shade-tolerant leaf shrub** · lifespan years (plant once,
cut for years; first leaf 90–120 d). Perennial and ever-growing — a long-cropping green, not a
placenta annual. Its deep-shade tolerance lets it hold a low stratum a food canopy already closed.

- **Temperature** — Ferns: preferred **20–32 °C → 68–90 °F**; tolerated **12–38 °C → 54–100 °F**.
- **Altitude** — Ferns: **not specified.** The copy's "coast to 4,300 ft" is **not from Ferns** — treat

- **Soil / pH** — Ferns: thrives on a wide range of soils; pH preferred **5.5–6.5**, tolerated 4.5–7.5.
- **Light** — Ferns: prefers sun **but also succeeds in deep shade** (larger leaves in partial shade) —
  unusual for a plant this productive.

## Propagation & timing — by source
- Ferns: stem cuttings (seed unreliable); dry the cut 1–14 days first so it seals. First harvest
  90–120 days. Up to ~12 t/ha dry leaf on good ground.

## Uses / food — by source
- Ferns: young leaves + shoots cooked (~25 % protein dry, real iron + calcium). **Cook, never raw.**

## Hazards — by source
- Ferns: **raw leaves carry cyanogenic glycosides → hydrogen cyanide; ~15 min cooking detoxifies**
  (a 5-min cook handles a normal serving; nobody eats it raw). White sap irritates skin on long
  contact → gloves. Wild forms sting; cultivated forms usually unarmed. Do not cook in aluminium.
  Hazard is **frame-dependent** (dose + raw prep) — held at true size: cook it and eat it.

## Inferences
- Deep-shade tolerance + cut-for-years habit makes it one of the few greens that keeps producing
  *under* a closing canopy — a genuine understory food layer, not just an open-ground crop.

## Anecdotes
- Yucatán Maya dooryard plant, grown beside the house for ~2,000 years, almost never as a field crop
  — a few plants by the door feed a family a weekly green. The chili/aibika pattern.
