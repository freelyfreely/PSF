ʻUlu · Breadfruit

Artocarpus altilis

Fruit, starch · Wet · Warm · Low · Sun

A tree that makes bread. One mature ʻulu drops more starch in a season than a family eats, on ground that gets no fertilizer and no tilling. Slow for three years, then it feeds for fifty.

Prep

Wet ground, warm, low. Happiest near the coast and thinning out well before the mountain.

No frost. One cold night undoes a year.

Drainage is the one thing it won't forgive. Standing water rots the roots and a three-year tree goes over.

Thirty feet of room. The roots run shallow and wide, so the digging under it stops once it's in.

Set

Grafted, not seed. Seed takes five to ten years and comes up a stranger. A grafted tree fruits in two to four.

Goes in during the rains, set slightly high so it drains.

Shade for the first year or two, then open sun. A banana on the windward side gives both shade and shelter.

Slow the first three years. Nothing is wrong.

Forget

Fruits May to October, then again. Two crops most years.

Leaves the size of a doormat, falling all year. That is the mulch. It stays where it lands.

Tip-prune the new growth once a year after harvest. Left alone it goes eighty feet. Cut back it stays at twenty and the fruit stays in reach.

By year five the shade underneath is deep enough for kalo.

Picked green and firm it cooks like a potato, floury and plain, taking on whatever it is cooked with. Left to ripen it turns sweet, nearly banana, and roasts to caramel. Two foods from one tree, a week apart.

The skin goes dull yellow-green and a thumb-press gives just slightly. Cut the stalk and watch the sap: milky means wait, watery means now. Early morning, before the latex turns to glue. Rinse it right away. Never raw. Boil, roast, bake, or fry. The seeded kinds carry seeds that cook up like chestnuts.

Maiʻa on the windward side while it establishes.

Kalo underneath once the canopy closes, around year three to five. It wants exactly the humid shade ʻulu makes.

Something nitrogen-fixing in between. ʻUlu takes and does not give that back.

Cacao or coffee under it, or beside it while it is young and still letting light through.

Seed goes in a little away from the trunk. The leaf litter is unfriendly to seedlings.

ʻUlu came by canoe. Polynesian voyagers carried it two and a half thousand miles across open ocean because a family with an ʻulu tree does not starve. Kū turned himself into one to feed his own. Every seedless tree on this island is a cutting of a cutting of a cutting, back to New Guinea. The same tree was later loaded onto the Bounty to feed enslaved people cheaply in the Caribbean. It has fed people who chose it and people who were given no choice. The bark made cloth, the sap caulked the hull, the wood made the canoe it arrived in.

ʻUlu · Breadfruit

Artocarpus altilis

Fruit, starch · Canopy · First harvest year three

A tree that makes bread. Grafted, it fruits around year three and keeps going past a lifetime. Fifty to two hundred fruits a season, twice a year, from ground that gets nothing but its own fallen leaves.

Prep

Wet, warm, low ground. No frost, ever. Drainage is the one thing it won't forgive.

Thirty feet of room. The roots run shallow and wide.

Set

Grafted, not seed. Seed takes ten years and comes up a stranger.

Goes in during the rains. Wants shade its first year or two. A banana on the windward side gives that and takes the wind off it.

Forget

Fruits May to October, and again. The leaves fall all year and stay where they land.

Tip-prune once a year and the fruit stays in reach. By year five the shade is deep enough for kalo.
