Pigeon pea

Cajanus cajan

Peas — protein wet or dry warm coast to 3,000 ft sun

A bean bush that makes its own fertilizer, and yours. It gives peas in six months and gives the soil nitrogen the whole time it stands. You will cut it down in year five to feed the tree beside it. That was always the plan, and it is not a loss.

first peas
1 3 5 10 50

Prep

  • Poor ground is fine. Poor ground is the point. It will not thank you for compost, and it does its best work where nothing else will start.
  • Full sun. It is the tallest thing here for about two years, and then it isn't.
  • Four feet apart, and closer than feels right. You are planting a stand, not a specimen.
  • Put it where a tree is going. It is not the crop. It is the ground the crop will stand in.

Set

  • Seed, straight into the ground, a handful at a time. It costs almost nothing and comes up in a week.
  • Plant it the same day as the tree it is nursing. Not before, not after. The whole method is in that sentence.
  • No water once it is up. Its roots go down further than yours can dig.
  • Fast. It is knee-high in two months and over your head by the end of the year.

Forget

  • Peas from month six, and then whenever you want them. Take what you want. The plant is not counting.
  • Cut it back hard when it flowers. Half its height, and everything you cut stays on the ground where it lands. It is back within weeks, and it comes back stronger for it.
  • That cut is the whole transaction. It drops nitrogen from its roots at the same moment it drops leaves on the soil, and the tree beside it eats both. You are not maintaining a bush. You are feeding a tree with a knife.
  • By year five the ʻulu is over it and it starts to thin. Don't fight it and don't replace it. It leaves behind five years of mulch and a soil that can hold a tree, which is the only thing it was ever for.

The food

Green, they are a fresh pea. Shell them and cook them like any other, five minutes, no more. Left on the bush they dry hard in the pod, and dry is how most of the world eats them: soaked overnight, simmered soft, into rice and into dal. A stand of six bushes will keep a household in protein without being asked. The young leaves go into soup. The old stems burn hot and clean, so the prunings you don't leave down become the fire you cook the peas on.

You have already seen this plant. It stands in the gravel at the edge of the highway, in the empty lot behind the store, on every piece of ground somebody stopped mowing. Most people here look at it and see scrub. Half the world looks at it and sees dinner: it is gandule in Puerto Rico, toor dal across India, rice and peas at Christmas in Jamaica. It went out of Africa four thousand years ago and it has been feeding people the whole way, on the worst ground available, without being planted, watered, or thanked.

ʻUlu

Breadfruit

Artocarpus altilis

Fruit — starch wet warm, no frost coast to 1,500 ft sun

A tree that makes bread. One mature ʻulu drops more starch in a season than a family eats, on ground that gets no fertilizer and no tilling. It is slow for three years, and then it is the reason the rest of the garden works.

first fruit still going
1 3 5 10 50

Prep

  • Wet ground, warm, low. Best under 2,000 feet, happiest near the coast, and it thins out well before the mountain.
  • No frost. Ever. Below about 41°F the leaves burn, and one cold night undoes a year.
  • Drainage is the one thing it won't forgive. Standing water rots the roots and a three-year tree goes over.
  • Thirty feet of room, and a shallow, wide root system that ends the digging under it.

Set

  • Grafted, not seed. Seed takes five to ten years and comes up a stranger. A grafted tree fruits in two to four.
  • Plant in the rains. Set it slightly high so it drains.
  • Shade for the first year or two, then open sun. A banana on the windward side does both jobs, shade and windbreak, and feeds the soil while it does.
  • Slow the first three years. Nothing is wrong.

Forget

  • Fruits May to October, then again. Two crops most years, one big one some years.
  • Leaves the size of a doormat, dropping all year. That is the mulch. It stays where it falls.
  • Tip-prune the new growth once a year, after harvest. Left alone it goes eighty feet. Cut back it stays at twenty and the fruit stays in reach.
  • By year five the shade underneath is deep. That is not a problem. That is when the kalo and the cacao go in.

The food

Picked green and firm it cooks like a potato: floury, plain, takes on whatever it is cooked with. Left to ripen it turns sweet, nearly banana, and roasts to caramel. Two foods, one tree, and the difference is a week. The skin goes dull yellow-green and a thumb-press gives just slightly. Cut the stalk and watch the sap. Milky means wait, watery means now. Early morning, before the latex turns to glue. Rinse it right away. Never raw: boil, roast, bake, or fry. Seeds, on the seeded kinds, cook up like chestnuts.

ʻUlu came by canoe. Voyagers carried it two and a half thousand miles across open ocean because a family with an ʻulu tree does not starve, and Kū turned himself into one to feed his own. Every seedless tree on this island is a cutting of a cutting of a cutting, an unbroken line of hands back to New Guinea. The same tree was later loaded onto the Bounty to feed enslaved people cheaply in the Caribbean. It has fed people who chose it and people who were given no choice. Here it is still the first one. The bark made cloth, the sap caulked the hull, the wood made the canoe it arrived in.
